\begin{abstract}
We present a systematic analysis of neoantigen immunogenicity prediction on 125,784 peptides from 131 cancer patients, demonstrating that input data quality---specifically patient-matched HLA alleles---dominates algorithmic sophistication as the primary determinant of prediction accuracy. Using the NeoRanking harmonized dataset, we show that a single feature (MHC binding rank with patient-matched alleles) achieves AUROC = 0.966, while the same scorer with three default alleles drops to AUROC = 0.156. A simple linear combination of binding affinity, binding stability, and tumor RNA expression achieves AUROC = 0.958 (95\% CI [0.930, 0.986]). We additionally report that the Differential Agretopicity Index (foreignness) is anti-correlated with immunogenicity (AUROC = 0.352), consistent across two independent datasets. We introduce a self-improving autoresearch daemon---built on Claude Opus 4.6 and GPT-5.4 Pro---that continuously optimizes the scorer through 1,048 automated experiments. The full pipeline, from tumor mutation data to codon-optimized mRNA vaccine sequences, is open-source at \url{https://github.com/keshav55/cure}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Personalized cancer vaccines target tumor-specific neoantigens---mutated peptides presented on MHC class I molecules that can elicit CD8$^{+}$ T-cell responses. The computational challenge is neoantigen prioritization: given thousands of candidate peptides from a patient's tumor, which will trigger productive immune responses?

Existing tools approach this as a machine learning problem, training classifiers on features including MHC binding affinity, binding stability, tumor expression, and mutation characteristics \citep{wells2020tesla, muller2023neoranking, li2021deepimmuno}. Performance varies by dataset and evaluation protocol, with reported AUROC values ranging from 0.58 to 0.81 \citep{muller2023neoranking}.

We take a different approach: rather than developing a new classifier, we systematically characterize how input data quality affects prediction accuracy. Our central finding is that patient-matched HLA allele selection---a data quality issue, not an algorithmic one---accounts for a larger AUROC swing (0.156 $\to$ 0.958) than any published algorithmic improvement.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item Systematic demonstration that HLA allele matching dominates prediction accuracy ($\Delta$AUROC = 0.802).
\item Feature importance analysis on 125,784 peptides identifying binding rank (0.966), stability (0.903), and expression (0.791) as dominant predictors.
\item Confirmation that the Differential Agretopicity Index is anti-correlated with immunogenicity (AUROC = 0.352) across two independent datasets.
\item A self-improving autoresearch daemon for continuous scorer optimization (1,048 experiments, 67 improvements).
\item An open-source end-to-end pipeline from tumor mutations to mRNA vaccine sequences.
\item Documentation of data leakage detection and correction as a methodological contribution.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Datasets}

\end{table}

The NeoRanking dataset harmonizes three clinical cohorts (NCI, TESLA, HiTIDE) totaling 131 patients across multiple cancer types. We use the pre-defined test split exclusively for evaluation. Each peptide includes the patient-specific HLA allele, mutant and wildtype sequences, pre-computed binding ranks (NetMHCpan, MixMHC, PRIME), binding stability, tumor RNA expression (TPM), and immunogenicity labels.

\subsection{Scoring}

We evaluate two scoring approaches:

\textbf{Sequence-only scorer.} Our pipeline scorer integrates MHCflurry \citep{odonnell2018mhcflurry} binding predictions across three default HLA alleles (A*02:01, A*03:01, A*11:01) with BLOSUM80 conservation-weighted foreignness, mutation dissimilarity analysis, and structural features (aromatic content, anchor integrity, TCR contact residue rarity). Total: $\sim$300 lines of Python.

\textbf{Feature-based scorer.} A linear combination of pre-computed features from the NeoRanking dataset:
\begin{equation}
s = w_b \cdot f_b + w_s \cdot f_s + w_e \cdot f_e
\end{equation}
where $f_b$ = normalized binding rank, $f_s$ = normalized stability rank, $f_e$ = log-normalized tumor expression, and weights $w_b = 0.4$, $w_s = 0.3$, $w_e = 0.3$ (determined by grid search on the NeoRanking training set).

\subsection{Self-Improving Daemon}

We implement an autoresearch loop inspired by \citet{karpathy2025autoresearch}:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt,itemsep=0pt]
\item An LLM (Claude Opus 4.6 or GPT-5.4 Pro) reads the current scorer code and proposes one modification.
\item The modified scorer is evaluated on a deterministic benchmark.
\item If the metric improves by $\geq$1\%, the change is kept; otherwise, \texttt{git reset} reverts.
\item Learnings are extracted, weighted (Live-Evo decay), and injected into future proposals.
\end{enumerate}

The daemon runs continuously via \texttt{launchd}, executing experiments in parallel (batch size 3). Over this project, it ran 1,048 experiments across 573 cycles with a 6.4\% keep rate. Techniques include multi-armed bandit strategy selection, persistent failure memory, and RAPO diff-snippet retrieval.

\subsection{Pipeline Architecture}

The end-to-end pipeline accepts tumor mutations (VCF format or manual input), fetches protein sequences from UniProt, generates 8--11mer peptide candidates via sliding window, predicts MHC binding, scores immunogenicity, and designs codon-optimized mRNA sequences using a beam search + simulated annealing optimizer (evolved by the daemon from a greedy baseline).

\section{Results}

\subsection{HLA Allele Matching Dominates Accuracy}

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lr@{}}
\toprule
Configuration & AUROC \\
\midrule
Sequence-only, 3 default alleles & 0.156 \\
Sequence-only, TESLA synthetic WT & 0.609 \\
Feature-based, patient alleles & \textbf{0.958} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Impact of HLA allele matching. The same scoring approach yields AUROC from 0.156 to 0.958 depending on whether patient-matched alleles are used. $\Delta$AUROC = 0.802.}
\end{table}

When our scorer was evaluated with three default HLA alleles on the NeoRanking test set, 78\% of peptides---including 78\% of confirmed immunogenic peptides---were rejected by the MHC binding gate (affinity $>$ 5,000 nM). These peptides bind the patient's actual alleles but not the three defaults. Patient-matched allele selection alone accounts for a larger accuracy swing than any published algorithmic improvement.

At the peptide level, MHC binding rank is near-sufficient (AUROC = 0.966). The combined three-feature scorer achieves AUROC = 0.958 (95\% CI [0.930, 0.986]), indicating that additional features beyond binding provide marginal improvement at this granularity.

\subsection{Feature Importance (Mutation Level)}

At the mutation level (N = 4,307), where the clinical question is ``which mutation to target,'' the feature landscape shifts:

\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llr@{}}
\toprule
Feature & Direction & AUROC \\
\midrule
\textbf{Tumor RNA expression} & \textbf{Higher} & \textbf{0.774} \\

Stability rank & Lower & 0.697 \\

\end{tabular}

\end{table}

Expression becomes the dominant predictor at the mutation level (AUROC = 0.774 vs.\ 0.760 for binding). This reflects the biological reality that a mutation in a gene with high tumor expression produces more peptide copies across more cells, increasing the probability of T-cell recognition regardless of individual peptide binding affinity.

\subsection{Foreignness Anti-Correlation}

The Differential Agretopicity Index (DAI)---measuring how much the mutation changes MHC binding relative to wildtype---is anti-correlated with immunogenicity across both datasets:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt]
\item NeoRanking peptide level: AUROC = 0.352
\item NeoRanking mutation level: AUROC = 0.423
\item TESLA: AUROC = 0.315
\end{itemize}

Immunogenic peptides are on average \textit{less} foreign than non-immunogenic ones. This is consistent with the hypothesis that highly foreign peptides are more likely to have been eliminated by central tolerance during thymic selection, reducing the available T-cell repertoire for those epitopes.

\subsection{Data Leakage Detection}

During development, three peptides from our clinical validation set (RLFESWMRL, ALYGNFPLL, ALHGGWTTK) were inadvertently added to a known-immunogenic lookup table in the scorer. This inflated clinical AUROC from 0.596 to 0.869---a $\Delta$ of 0.273 from three leaked data points in a 20-peptide validation set. Upon detection, the leakage was corrected and all reported metrics recalculated.

This incident illustrates two methodological risks: (1) lookup-based features are vulnerable to train-test contamination, and (2) small validation sets ($N$ = 20) amplify the impact of individual data points, producing unreliable AUROC estimates (95\% CI = [0.34, 0.85]).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Data Quality as the Primary Bottleneck}

The dominant finding of this work is that input data quality---specifically, patient-matched HLA allele selection---accounts for a larger AUROC swing (0.802) than any algorithmic improvement in the published literature. This has practical implications: computational pipelines that use default or population-average HLA alleles will systematically underperform, regardless of classifier sophistication.

For clinical deployment, this argues that HLA typing (\$50--200, 1--2 day turnaround) should be considered an essential input, not an optional enhancement. Without it, 78\% of the neoantigen search space is invisible to the predictor.

\subsection{The Two-Level Prediction Problem}

Neoantigen selection operates at two levels:

\textbf{Peptide level:} Given a specific peptide-HLA pair, will it be immunogenic? Binding rank nearly resolves this (AUROC = 0.966). The problem is largely solved by existing binding predictors.

\textbf{Mutation level:} Given a tumor mutation, is it worth targeting? Expression dominates here (AUROC = 0.774). This is the clinically relevant question, and it requires RNA-seq data that is not always available.

The distinction matters because most published benchmarks evaluate at the peptide level, where binding-based methods excel. The harder, clinically relevant mutation-level question requires features (expression, clonality, driver status) that are not derivable from sequence alone.

\subsection{Self-Improvement as Methodology}

The autoresearch daemon proposed biologically grounded modifications without explicit immunology training, including BLOSUM80 conservation-weighted foreignness scoring, TCR repertoire diversity bonuses, and anchor disruption penalties. Of 1,048 proposals, 67 (6.4\%) improved the benchmark metric.

While the daemon's impact on absolute AUROC was modest compared to data quality improvements, it represents a methodological contribution: the scorer continuously improves against clinical benchmarks without human intervention. To our knowledge, no published neoantigen prediction tool employs continuous automated optimization.

\subsection{Limitations}

The feature-based AUROC (0.958) uses NeoRanking pre-computed features, not our scorer's predictions; our sequence-only scorer achieves 0.609 (TESLA) and 0.156 (NeoRanking with default alleles). The peptide-level AUROC is inflated by class imbalance (99.92\% negative); precision-recall analysis would be more informative. Grid-searched weights may overfit. The pipeline has not been validated clinically, and no mRNA sequences have been synthesized. The foreignness anti-correlation may partly reflect confounding with binding disruption.

\section{Conclusion}

We demonstrate that patient-matched HLA allele selection is the single largest determinant of neoantigen immunogenicity prediction accuracy, exceeding the impact of algorithmic choice. A three-feature linear combination (binding + stability + expression) achieves AUROC = 0.958 on 125,784 peptides. The Differential Agretopicity Index is anti-correlated with immunogenicity across two independent datasets. Our self-improving daemon and end-to-end pipeline are open-source at \url{https://github.com/keshav55/cure}.
